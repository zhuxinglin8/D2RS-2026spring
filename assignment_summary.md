# Assignment: Mineral-Enzyme Interactions in Permafrost

## Overview
This assignment analyzes the mechanisms of soil organic carbon (SOC) accumulation and stabilization in Tibetan Plateau (warm) and Alaskan (cold) permafrost.

## Key Findings
- **SOC Accumulation**: Alaskan tundra soils have higher SOC concentrations compared to Tibetan grasslands.
- **Microbial Necromass Carbon (MNC)**: 
    - MNC accounts for 44% of SOC in Tibetan grasslands, vs 10% in Alaskan tundra.
    - Fungal MNC plays a larger role in Alaskan tundra (10:1 ratio with bacterial MNC) compared to Tibetan grasslands (3:1 ratio).
- **Peroxidase Activity**:
    - Peroxidase activity is 8–21 times higher in Alaskan tundra than in Tibetan grasslands.
    - Higher peroxidase activity in Alaska correlates with higher SOC and iron-bound organic carbon (Fe-OC).
- **Mechanism**: 
    - Tibetan: SOC is primarily stabilized via organo-mineral associations.
    - Alaska: A fungal-driven pathway predominates, where peroxidase activity breaks down recalcitrant OM, creating reactive sites (etch pits) for mineral nanoparticle attachment.

## Conclusion
Peroxidase-driven H2O2 reduction is a previously unrecognized mechanism for SOC stabilization in Arctic permafrost, though it also increases the vulnerability of SOC to degradation under warming.
